1.4.1 there are complex interactions between the meaning of a biosignal, and way it is represented by the interface

  1. consider the spire breath data
    1. if you look at the raw breathing data over time,
      1. and you asked someone to tell you how risky that data was to disclose/how much it says about them
      2. they'd probably say "whatever."
    2. now, if you showed them the interpreted data (aclm, focused, etc…)
      1. they may still say i'ts not so risky to disclose, but maybe they'd be slightly less sure…..
  2. almost a consumer rights issue
    1. companies can represent data in a way that makes them seem less risky to disclose/share
  3. unless we know what the company's algorithms tell them,
    1. we cant know our own risk
  4. my bet is, people undervalue the threat of unknown signals,
    1. and/or make incorrect assumptions about what algorithms can know from their biosensor data. this is something companies can exploit to their distinct advantage.

1.4.2 an epistomological point to be made here

  1. people's interpretations of biosignals come from a complex web of knowledge
    1. cultural
    2. drawn from lived bodily experiences
  2. i am not really interested in untangling this web
    1. in fact, we can acknowledge that the the meaning of information is inextricable from its representation,
      1. including the "materiality" of that representation.
  3. HOWEVER! we can show that changing the representation + signal independently produces diverse effects
    1. that is, neither representation nor signal 100% predicts the response
      1. if i take a reperesentation
      2. vary the signal
      3. and see the same interpretations
      4. i would be dispapointed
